10 Strategies Tested

A personal research project in systematic trading, built from scratch as a solo effort. I designed the full pipeline—from hypothesis generation and data infrastructure to statistical validation and live deployment—treating each strategy as an empirical research question: formulate a thesis, design a fair test, and accept the result regardless of outcome.

Of 10 strategies investigated across a 99-ticker universe, most were rejected after rigorous out-of-sample testing. The two that survived were validated through bootstrap confidence intervals, outlier-dependence analysis, and walk-forward evaluation with realistic transaction costs before being deployed to live trading.

Opening Range Momentum / Reversal

Deployed

Thesis: The opening bar carries a directional signal that can be traded as either momentum or reversal, depending on the ticker and regime.

I designed a systematic search across two intraday timeframes, then applied a multi-phase validation pipeline to control for overfitting: nested train/validation splits, bootstrap confidence interval filtering (95% CI excluding zero), winsorized outlier-dependence checks, and rolling stability tests. Of 49 initial candidates, 11 survived all filters—these are the configs currently trading live.

A key finding was that the signal contains genuine directional content (97% of losing trades moved favorably before exit), identifying exit engineering as the primary research frontier. I also showed that adaptive config selection (re-optimizing monthly) destroys edge vs. holding fixed configs—a non-obvious result that shaped the deployment strategy.

Candlestick Rejection + Momentum Extremes

Edge Found

Thesis: Combining a price-action signal (candlestick rejection) with a regime filter (momentum extremes) produces a mean-reversion edge that neither signal achieves alone.

This strategy emerged from a deliberate research progression: after the standalone rejection signal failed (see below), I hypothesized that adding contextual confirmation would rescue the signal. Testing across 97 tickers confirmed the hypothesis—9 passed with statistically significant positive expected value (p = 0.0001, 57% win rate), validated through jackknife stability checks and multi-year rolling evaluation.

Candlestick Rejection (Standalone)

No Edge

Thesis: Candles with dominant wicks signal institutional rejection and predict short-term direction.

Tested extensively across 99 tickers with multiple signal quality gates. The result was unambiguous: no directional content in isolation. However, this negative result directly motivated the combination strategy above—the signal needed contextual filtering, not better parameterization.

Mean-Reversion Dip

No Edge

Thesis: Intraday pullbacks from recent highs create mean-reversion opportunities.

Tested across multiple lookback windows and reversion thresholds on 99 tickers. While a weak signal existed pre-cost, realistic transaction costs eliminated profitability for nearly all configurations—a pattern that recurred across several strategies and reinforced cost modeling as a first-order design constraint.

Fair Value Gap Pairs

No Edge

Thesis: Sequential fair value gaps (same or opposite direction) carry predictive information about continuation or reversal.

Analyzed 25.8M gap pairs across 41 tickers. The favorable/adverse excursion ratio was 0.999—statistically indistinguishable from random. This clean null result ruled out an entire class of gap-based signals with high confidence.

Liquidity Sweep

No Edge

Thesis: Price wicks that sweep key structural levels (prior-day high/low, opening range) predict directional continuation.

Designed a paired-delta methodology comparing each sweep event against matched controls (same time-of-day, similar VWAP displacement and volatility) to isolate the sweep-specific effect. With 2.5M+ observations, the test had sufficient power to detect edges as small as 0.2 bps—none were found. Sweeps are real price events but carry no tradeable directional information.

Stochastic Mean-Reversion

No Edge

Thesis: Modeling intraday price as an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process enables systematic mean-reversion entries at extreme deviations.

Iterated through three research phases, progressively refining entry gates and exit logic. Each phase diagnosed specific failure modes (cost sensitivity, regime dependence, parameter instability) and attempted targeted fixes. Ultimately, the signal-to-noise ratio at intraday timescales was insufficient to overcome transaction costs—a principled conclusion after exhaustive investigation.

VWAP Crossover

No Edge

Thesis: VWAP crossings with sufficient divergence signal institutional flow and predict short-term direction.

Comprehensive parameter sweep across signal and exit configurations on 41 tickers. No post-cost edge found, consistent with the broader finding that single-indicator signals lack sufficient edge in liquid US equities.
